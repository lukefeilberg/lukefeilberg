Getting the hours open in a day¶

Note that the minutes are converted to hours as well (by dividing by 60) so that, for example, 1.5 hours is of course 1 hours 30 minutes.

In [7]:
def time_open(row):
    
    total_time = 0.0
    
    # All DMV's in this dataset open before 12 so subtracting from 12 gives hours open before 12.
    # h_open standing for "hour open" and m_open for "minute_open".
    h_open,m_open = re.split(r':', row['opens'])
    total_time += 12 - (float(h_open)+float(m_open)/60)
    
    # All DMV's in this dataset close after 12 so we add to the total time simply the hour and its minutes
    h_close,m_close = re.split(r':', row['closes'])
    total_time += float(h_close) + float(m_close)/60
    
    # Checking if the DMV closes for lunch
    if row['lunch_starts'] is not np.nan:
        lunch_time = 0.0
        
        lh_start,lm_start = re.split(r':', row['lunch_starts'])
        lunch_time += 12 - (float(lh_start)+float(lm_start)/60)
        
        # Here we must mod 12 as some DMV lunches end at, for example, 12:30
        lh_ends,lm_ends = re.split(r':', row['lunch_ends'])
        lunch_time += float(lh_ends)%12 + float(lm_ends)/60
        
        total_time -= lunch_time
    
    return total_time

We'll now run the following regression:

$$\text{turnout}_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1\text{total_hours}_i + u_i$$¶

With the following null and alternative hypotheses:

$$H_0: \beta_1 = 0, H_A: \beta_1 \neq 0 $$¶

PART 9: DISCUSSION¶

We see that including extra covariates did not alter the coeffecient estimate of our first variable of interest, total_hours. Quite counterintuitively, our results indicate that for each extra hour DMV's are open in a county, voter turnout actually decreases by 0.07 percentage points. This is a rather small effect as the voter turnout data had a sample standard deviation of approximately 4.68 percentage points, further it is not significant at traditional levels. Nonetheless, perhaps counties with low voter turnout have been targeted with longer DMV hours.

The second variable of interest, distance, which measures the average distance to the closest DMV for a sample of low income housing units, indicates that for each mile further away the nearest DMV is (to low-income housing) voter turnout decreases by 0.19 percentage points. So if, say, a DMV closed down and the next nearest one open was 10 miles further, we'd expect voter turnout to drop by 1.9 percentage points, a bit less than half a standard deviation. However, this estimate is not significant at traditional levels as well.

As for the other covariates, counties with higher percentage of college graduates and higher unemployment rates are associated with higher voter turnout, where both estimates are significant at the 95% level. Lastly the percent black/African American a county is is associated with a slight decrease in voter turnout, however it has a p-value of 0.72 so is not at all significant.

Overall, in this context there has been no significant evidence of voter suppression found. There's a number of possible explanations as well as much room for improvement. For one, it is possible that counties with low voter turnout have since been targeted with longer DMV hours, which if true would explain the countintuitive sign on our estimate. Further, this analysis leaves out DMV's which have closed down, which perhaps could be specified as having 0 hours and included in the average DMV hours. The distance to DMV variable was chosen rather ad hoc and different measures of DMV distance like, say, distance from the county center may be favored. In fact, percent of a county riding public transport may be more informative and capture better the difficulty of obtaining an ID to vote, however I've been unable to find such data at the county level in Alabama.
